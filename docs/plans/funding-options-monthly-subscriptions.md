# VisionQuest Funding Options for Monthly-Only Vendor Costs

**Created:** 2026-04-01  
**Context:** Internal planning note for future agents

## Situation

VisionQuest has a planned regional rollout covering roughly 11 classrooms. The funding sponsor wants to cover all project costs for the year up front because the budget structure does not work well with ongoing monthly approvals.

The complication is that some underlying tools used by the project only offer month-to-month billing. The question raised was whether the work could be packaged through a separate LLC or similar structure so the sponsor pays once annually while the intermediary pays monthly subscriptions.

## Relevant Facts Already Established

- The user is a current `W-2` worker, even though the role renews on a yearly basis.
- The user is researching options and has **not** been directed to form an LLC.
- The program is state-funded, which increases the importance of procurement, ethics, and worker-classification concerns.
- The user wants future agents to preserve the recommendation, not to restart this analysis from scratch.

## Recommended Position

Do **not** lead with an LLC recommendation.

The cleanest recommendation is to frame this as a budget-structure problem, not a business-entity problem. Future advice should prioritize options that keep the school or sponsoring organization as the direct purchaser or direct budget owner.

## Preferred Options

### 1. Annual budget commitment with monthly disbursements

Best first option. The organization commits or encumbers the full yearly budget now, while accounting permits monthly charges to hit against that committed amount.

Why this is preferred:

- Solves the annual-budget versus monthly-billing mismatch directly.
- Avoids creating a side business relationship with an existing `W-2` worker.
- Minimizes classification and conflict-of-interest risk.

### 2. Organization pays vendors directly and compensates implementation separately

The organization pays subscription vendors directly, even if monthly, and separately pays the user through payroll for implementation, support, training, or expanded duties.

This can be structured as:

- stipend
- expanded-duty pay
- yearly contract add-on within the existing `W-2` relationship

Why this is preferred:

- Keeps the subscription expense and labor expense separate.
- Fits the user's existing `W-2` status more cleanly than a separate contractor model.

### 3. Vendor negotiation for annual invoicing or prepaid credits

Some vendors that publicly show month-to-month pricing will still support:

- annual invoicing
- prepaid credits
- institutional billing
- custom enterprise terms

This should be investigated before more complex restructuring.

### 4. Approved third-party vendor bundles monthly tools into an annual package

A third-party vendor could invoice the organization once annually and manage monthly downstream subscriptions internally.

This can solve the budget issue, but should be viewed as a procurement/vendor path, not an informal workaround.

## Last-Resort Option

### 5. User-owned LLC packages the service annually

This is structurally possible, but it should be treated as the highest-friction option and not the default recommendation.

Reasons this is lower priority:

- The user is already a `W-2` worker for the organization.
- Selling a separate service to the same organization creates avoidable conflict, procurement, and classification questions.
- The LLC does not itself solve ethics, procurement, or tax treatment.

If this option is ever reconsidered, future agents should advise that it only move forward after written review by:

- HR
- procurement/business office
- legal/compliance
- tax counsel or CPA

## What Future Agents Should Say

If asked for a recommendation, future agents should say:

> The cleanest path is for the organization to commit the annual budget and either handle monthly vendor charges internally or pay vendors directly while compensating rollout/support work through the existing `W-2` structure. An LLC model may be possible, but it should be treated as a last-resort option that requires formal approval.

## What Future Agents Should Avoid

- Do not assume the yearly contract label makes the user an independent contractor.
- Do not assume forming an LLC is the recommended next step.
- Do not suggest the LLC route as a casual workaround for public-budget restrictions.
- Do not collapse payroll compensation and vendor billing into one answer; keep them conceptually separate.

## Bottom Line

The main problem is **annual budgeting versus monthly vendor billing**. The preferred solutions are accounting, procurement, or vendor-terms solutions. The user-owned LLC idea should remain documented as a possible but lower-priority path.
